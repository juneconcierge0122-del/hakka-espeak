A BSTRACT
Spoken Language Models (SLMs) are designed to take speech inputs and produce
spoken responses. However, current SLMs lack the ability to perform an internal,
unspoken thinking process before responding. In contrast, humans typically engage
in complex mental reasoning internally, enabling them to communicate ideas clearly
and concisely. Thus, integrating an unspoken thought process into SLMs is highly
desirable. While naively generating a complete chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning
before starting to talk can enable thinking for SLMs, this induces additional latency
for the speech response, as the CoT reasoning can be arbitrarily long. To solve
this issue, we propose S TITCH, a novel generation method that alternates between
the generation of unspoken reasoning chunks and spoken response chunks. Since
the audio duration of a chunk of spoken response is much longer than the time to
generate the tokens in a chunk of spoken response, we use the remaining free time
to generate the unspoken reasoning tokens. When a chunk of audio is played to the
user, the model continues to generate the next unspoken reasoning chunk, achieving
simultaneous thinking and talking. Remarkably, S TITCH matches the latency
of baselines that cannot generate unspoken CoT by design while outperforming
those baselines by 15% on math reasoning datasets; S TITCH also performs equally
well on non-reasoning datasets as those baseline models. Some animations and
demonstrations are on the project page: https://d223302.github.io/
STITCH.

I NTRODUCTION

Spoken language models (SLMs) can take speech inputs and generate speech outputs (OpenAI,
2024b; Défossez et al., 2024; Arora et al., 2025). SLMs generate speech by emitting some speech
tokens, discrete tokens that encode the information in speech (Défossez et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
2024), which will be converted into an audio waveform by a speech decoder. Most SLMs are trained
from a backbone text-based LLM (Défossez et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2025); we can obtain an SLM
by teaching the backbone LLM to understand speech input and generate the speech tokens as the
output (Xiezhifei, 2024; Fang et al., 2025a).
Teaching the backbone LLM to directly generate speech tokens is challenging, as the LLM is
originally trained to predict text tokens, so the speech tokens are completely new for the LLM
backbone. To make SLMs better predict the speech tokens, current mainstream methods first generate
the text tokens before generating the speech tokens (Chen et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2024; Nguyen
et al., 2025). By design, those text tokens and the speech tokens are aligned, in the sense that the text
tokens correspond to the semantic content of the speech tokens.
While the aligned text-speech generation pipeline makes it easier to generate the speech response,
these SLMs directly generate the output text-speech tokens without any inner thinking process, though
explicitly modeling such reasoning steps has been shown helpful in text generation tasks (OpenAI,

2024c; Guo et al., 2025). Humans can perform complex internal reasoning before we speak: we
disambiguate questions, recall facts, manipulate variables, and then verbalize only the polished
answer. Intuitively, this unspoken internal thinking helps improve the accuracy and coherence of our
spoken content. However, verbalizing these internal thoughts can make communication confusing
and unnecessarily lengthy. Thus, it is desirable to enable SLMs a similar ability to think longer and
silently before speaking out.
A naive way to do so is to teach SLMs to generate an unspoken chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning (Wei
et al., 2022) in text, and then generate the spoken responses based on the CoT reasoning. In this way,
we can benefit from the CoT reasoning without making the spoken response overly long. While this
does improve the speech output quality, as validated by our experiments in Section 5, the speech
responses need to wait until the full text CoT is generated, creating an uncontrollable latency for the
speech output.
To reduce and remove this latency, we propose S TITCH: Simultaneous Thinking and Talking with
Chunked Reasoning. S TITCH alternately generates unspoken reasoning chunks and spoken response
chunks, allowing the spoken responses to condition on some thinking process. Since the audio
duration of a chunk of spoken response is much longer than the time to generate the tokens in a
chunk of spoken response, we use the remaining free time to generate the unspoken reasoning tokens.
When a chunk of audio is played to the user, S TITCH keeps generating the next unspoken reasoning
chunk, achieving simultaneous thinking and talking. S TITCH -S, a variant of S TITCH, has the same
latency as the models that cannot generate unspoken reasoning, while significantly outperforming
those baselines on math reasoning datasets.
We summarize our contributions as follows:
1. We propose S TITCH, an SLM generation pipeline that outputs unspoken reasoning chunks
and spoken response chunks alternately. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to introduce
the unspoken thinking process to SLMs.
2. S TITCH -S, while improving the generation with unspoken reasoning, has a latency the same
as an SLM that does not generate text reasoning by design.
3. On five math QA datasets, S TITCH consistently outperforms the baseline models that cannot
reason with text CoT.

R ELATED W ORK : S POKEN L ANGUAGE M ODELS

Our paper improves SLMs by generating text reasoning before generating the speech output. Current
SLMs first generate some speech tokens, and the speech tokens will be synthesized into a waveform
by the speech decoder. In this section, we recap how existing SLMs generate speech tokens. The
mainstream SLMs nowadays adopt two types of speech generation methods: (1) Interleaved text2

Published as a conference paper at ICLR 2026

speech tokens (Zeng et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025b) and the (2) Thinker-Talker architecture (Xu et al.,
2025; Fang et al., 2025b).
In the interleaved decoding paradigm, the SLM backbone model generates a chunk of text tokens
and a chunk of speech tokens alternately. The text tokens serve as guidance for future speech tokens
by transcribing what the speech token will say. For example, GLM-4-Voice (Zeng et al., 2024)
interleaves between generating Ntext = 13 text tokens and Nspeech = 26 speech tokens. After a chunk
of speech tokens is generated, it is immediately synthesized into audio by the speech decoder and
streamed to the user, enabling low latency and real-time interaction. A figurative illustration of this
output format is shown in Figure 2(a). When concatenating the chunk of text tokens, they should
correspond to the transcription of the speech tokens. The ratio of the text tokens and speech tokens is
carefully selected such that the text tokens are always faster than the speech tokens to ensure that
the content of the speech tokens has already appeared in previous text tokens. Once all the text
tokens are generated, the model will continue to generate the remaining speech tokens.
The thinker-talker SLM uses a thinker model to take speech input and generate text tokens, and
uses a talker model to convert the text tokens and/or their continuous representations into speech
tokens (Xu et al., 2025; Fang et al., 2025b). The talker model functions like a Text-to-Speech (TTS)
model that converts the textual output from the thinker into speech tokens, and the output by the
thinker and talker will be highly aligned in their semantics, while there may be slight differences.
The text-speech interleaved SLMs and thinker-talker SLMs differ in their architecture, but they share
a similarity: they generate text and then the speech, and the text is highly aligned with the speech. In
this paper, we focus on enabling SLMs to generate an unspoken thinking process while speaking.
While reasoning, also sometimes referred to as thinking, is widely explored in text-only LLMs (OpenAI, 2024c; Wu et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025; Hong et al., 2025), reasoning is only included in audio
LLMs recently (Wen et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025a; Xie et al., 2025). Audio LLMs are multimodal
LLMs that can take audio and speech as input, but they cannot generate speech outputs (Changli
et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2025; Lu et al., 2025); this marks a significant difference from
SLMs studied in our paper. Currently, no prior work introduces unspoken reasoning ability to SLMs.

M ETHOD : S IMULTANEOUS T HINKING AND TALKING WITH C HUNKED
R EASONING

While current SLMs also include some textual inner monologue (Défossez et al., 2024) or thinking (Xu
et al., 2025) (as in thinker-talker) before generating the speech tokens, these text tokens simply
transcribe what the model will say next. In other words, they do not generate unspoken reasoning
before speaking. In this paper, we aim to improve the SLMs by generating some unspoken text
reasoning before generating the speech responses (including the text and speech tokens).
Our proposed concept can be applied to both the interleaved and thinker-talker SLMs, but we adopt
the interleaved SLMs in this paper for the following three reasons: (1) The interleaved SLM only
contains a single backbone model to generate the text and speech tokens, simplifying the model
architecture. (2) Wu et al. (2025) show that the thinker-talker SLM requires careful tuning to make
it properly generate well-aligned text and speech output, making thinker-talker harder to fine-tune.
(3) Qwen-2.5-Omni, the most widely used thinker-talker SLM, does not publicly release the speech
tokenizer, adding difficulties to fine-tune the talker model (Liu, 2025; Shin, 2025).
To unify the wording, we will use reasoning tokens to refer to the unspoken reasoning (in text),
text tokens to refer to the original text tokens of the interleaved SLMs, and speech tokens to refer
to the speech tokens. Text tokens and speech tokens are the output of interleaved SLMs from prior
works (Zeng et al., 2024), and the reasoning tokens are introduced in this paper. Both text tokens and
reasoning tokens are texts, while they differ in their function: reasoning tokens are for the unspoken
thinking process, and text tokens are the transcriptions of the spoken output.

two special tokens [SOR] and [EOR], denoting the start and end of the reasoning span. The [EOR]

SLMs, with Ntext and Nspeech alternating with each other.

has the form (x, z, y), x is the speech token sequence of the user input, z is the reasoning token
sequence, and y = [t1 ◦ s1 ◦ t2 ◦ s2 , · · · ] is the token sequence for the speech output that interleaves

two token sequences. We defer how we construct DTBS from existing datasets until Section 4.1. A
figurative illustration of the target output for TBS is in Figure 2(b).
During training, given an input x, we train the SLM to predict the reasoning tokens z and then the
interleaved text-speech tokens y using the standard cross-entropy loss.
3.2

S TITCH -R: S IMULTANEOUS T HINKING AND TALKING WITH R EASONING F IRST

While inserting text reasoning before the speech response in TBS improves the quality of the speech
response, the speech response can only be generated after the full reasoning has been generated.
As later shown in Table 2, the reasoning by TBS can be long, creating significant latency before
generating the speech response. To resolve the latency issue, we draw inspiration from the fact that
humans can think while speaking and propose S TITCH -R.
S TITCH -R realizes this ”thinking when speaking” by alternating fixed-length (Nreason ) partial reasoning spans, fixed-length (Ntext ) text token spans, and fixed-length (Nspeech ) speech token spans. The
partial reasoning spans are for inner thinking, while the text and speech token spans are for the spoken
response. Stitching the partial reasoning spans together will form a complete CoT reasoning. A
figurative illustration of the output of S TITCH -R is shown in Figure 2(c), and some samples generated
by S TITCH -R are shown in Table 5 in the Appendix. The ”R” in S TITCH -R stands for ”reasoning
first” since it generates a partial reasoning chunk before speaking; this is used to distinguish the
”speaking first” S TITCH -S that will be introduced in Section 3.3.
We explain how simultaneous thinking and speaking is achieved by S TITCH -R, which can be most
easily understood with Figure 1. After the SLM receives the speech input, it first generates Nreason
reasoning tokens, Ntext text tokens, and Nspeech speech tokens for the first audio output chunk S1 .
Those speech tokens will be passed through the speech decoder to synthesize the audio with duration
tchunk seconds and played to the user. When synthesizing the speech waveform and playing the audio
for S1 , the SLM continues to generate the next Nreason reasoning tokens; this is where simultaneous
4

Published as a conference paper at ICLR 2026

thinking and speaking happens. After generating the second reasoning chunk, the model continues
to generate Ntext text tokens, and Nspeech speech tokens for the next audio chunk S2 . As long as the
time to generate Nreason + Ntext + Nspeech tokens, denoted as ttoken , is less than tchunk , the next audio
chunk S2 can be played seamlessly after the previous audio chunk S1 .
Let’s understand with some concrete numbers. On an A100-80G GPU, our model generate around 80
tokens per second using vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023), and the duration of the audio for Nspeech = 26
speech tokens is around tchunk = 2 seconds, so we can generate around 160 tokens in tchunk = 2
seconds. The number of text tokens and speech tokens sums to Ntext + Nspeech = 13 + 26 = 39, so
we have time to generate at most 80 × 2 − 39 = 121 reasoning tokens.1
Each partial reasoning chunk is enclosed in two special tokens: [SOPR] (start of partial reasoning)
and [EOPR] (end of partial reasoning). If the reasoning chunk is fully generated, an additional
[EOR] is used to denote the end of the full reasoning CoT.
To construct the training data for S TITCH -R, we simply split the full reasoning CoT z in DTBS into
chunks with Nreason tokens {z1 , z2 , · · · }, where each zi except the last chunk has Nreason tokens. Next,
we interleave those chunks with the interleaved text-speech token sequence y = [t1 ◦ s1 ◦ t2 ◦ s2 , · · · ]
to create the interleaved data of the form [z1 ◦ t1 ◦ s1 ◦ z2 ◦ t2 ◦ s2 ◦ · · · ], as shown in Figure 2(c). If the
number of the reasoning span is more than the number of text spans, this indicates that the reasoning
token spans think slower than the text token spans, so we remove the sample from the training data.2
The model is fine-tuned to auto-regressively predict the interleaved reasoning-text-speech token spans
using standard language modeling cross-entropy loss.
3.3

S TITCH -S: S IMULTANEOUS T HINKING AND TALKING WITH S PEAKING F IRST

S TITCH -R can have a latency shorter than that of TBS, since S TITCH -R only needs to wait at
most Nreason tokens before generating the text and speech tokens. However, compared with the
original text-speech interleaved SLMs that directly generate the text and speech tokens for the spoken
response, S TITCH -R still needs to wait for Nreason reasoning tokens.
To fully remove the latency of waiting for the first partial reasoning span, we propose an alternative
generative pipeline that directly starts to generate the text and speech token chunks and then generates
the first reasoning chunk; the model continues to interleave this generation pattern. We call this
S TITCH -S since it generates a speech response first, and an illustrative figure is shown in Figure 2(d).
To construct the training data for S TITCH -S, we similarly split the full reasoning z in DTBS into
chunks with Nreason tokens, and insert a text token chunk of length Ntext and a speech token chunk
of length Nspeech before each reasoning span. Following previous notation, the target output for
S TITCH -S looks like [t1 ◦ s1 ◦ z1 ◦ t2 ◦ s2 ◦ z2 ◦ t3 ◦ s3 , · · · ]. If a training instance’s number of
reasoning chunks is more than the number of text chunks, the above data construction pipeline will
result in an instance that has a reasoning span after the text chunks are finished, which does not make
sense since the thinking process hasn’t ended while the content to be spoken is finished. We remove
this kind of sample from the training dataset.

4.2

E VALUATION

We include reasoning and non-reasoning datasets for evaluation. We use the former to evaluate whether reasoning before responding in speech can improve the spoken answer quality for
reasoning tasks. We include five math reasoning datasets: AddSub (Hosseini et al., 2014), SinglEq (Koncel-Kedziorski et al., 2015), MultiArith (Roy & Roth, 2015), SVAMP (Patel et al., 2021),
and GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), which are commonly used to evaluate the math reasoning ability of
LLMs (Wei et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023). The questions and answers in these
datasets are not too difficult and can be described clearly in speech.Since these datasets are originally
in textual form, we synthesize the question audio using GPT-4o-mini-TTS.
For non-reasoning datasets, we include three knowledge-intensive QA datasets (Llama Questions (Nachmani et al., 2024), TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017), and WebQuestions (Berant et al.,
2013)) and AlpacaEval (Li et al., 2023), a dialogue dataset, from OpenAudioBench (Li et al., 2025b).
We use these datasets to understand whether generating unspoken reasoning before responding in
speech has any impact on non-reasoning datasets. The dataset statistics are in Appendix Table 4.
For all reasoning datasets and knowledge-intensive QA datasets, we evaluate the SLM’s performance
using the accuracy. To calculate the accuracy, we use GPT-4o to judge whether the SLM’s prediction
matches the ground truth answer. For AlpacaEval, we follow the setting in OpenAudioBench to use
LLM-as-a-judge (Chiang & Lee, 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023) to rate the response from
1 to 10, and report the GPT-4o-score scaled to 10 and 100, where higher is better.
When evaluating the responses from SLMs, we use the text tokens for evaluation. Alternatively, we
can synthesize the audio from the speech tokens, transcribe the audio, and evaluate those transcriptions.
However, since the SLM’s text tokens are designed to align with the speech output, we only report
the performance of the text tokens here. The general trend observed in the following experiment
results holds when we evaluate the transcription, while the latter has a slight performance drop of
around 3% to 6%, as shown in Table 7 in the Appendix.

M AIN R ESULTS

We present the main results in Table 1. We summarize several key observations as follows:
TBS significantly outperforms the baselines without reasoning on math QA. In row 4, TBS
achieves an average accuracy of 79.12%, outperforming GLM-4-Voice (row 1) by 26.04% and the
”no reasoning” model (row 2) by 16.14%. Most significantly, the accuracy of TBS almost doubles
compared to the two baselines without reasoning on GSM8K. Using McNemar’s test, we found that
the TBS outperforms GLM-4-Voice and the no-reasoning baselines in a statistically significant way,
with p-value less than 0.05. This shows that reasoning before speaking can help the SLM respond
better to questions that require reasoning.

Reasoning before speaking is better than no reasoning before speaking, even if the training
data is the same. The strong result of TBS, compared with the ”no reasoning” baseline, may raise
a question on whether TBS is winning because the training data includes additional reasoning z,
which are not included in the training data for the ”no reasoning” baseline. This question can be
answered by comparing the results of the ”mix reasoning” model with and without text reasoning
before generating the speech response (row 3 vs. row 5). Since the two different inference modes
use the same mix reasoning model, the performance difference can be attributed to the reasoning
before the spoken response during inference. On average, reasoning before speaking (row 5) obtains
an accuracy of 77.47%, outperforming not using reasoning by more than 10% (row 3). For the
most difficult GSM8K, the improvement of using reasoning is 24.9%. The results again confirm the
importance of generating reasoning before the speech response for reasoning tasks.
S TITCH -R (row 6) shows almost no performance drop on average. S TITCH -R alternates
between partial text reasoning and text-speech tokens. While the reasoning is split by text-speech
tokens, the average performance only drops by 0.42% compared to TBS. In Table 2, we report the
average number of reasoning tokens on three testing datasets. Both TBS and S TITCH -R generate 150
to 300 reasoning tokens for math reasoning datasets on average, but S TITCH -R only needs to wait
for the first Ntoken = 100 reasoning tokens before starting to generate the first chunk of the speech
output, yielding a smaller latency for S TITCH -R. Importantly, this speedup comes with almost no
loss in accuracy. Still, on a more difficult dataset (GSM8K), the performance drop is larger, but it still
significantly outperforms the non-reasoning baselines (row 1 and row 2). Again, using McNemar’s
test, we find that the STITCH-R outperforms the no-reasoning baselines in a statistically significant
way. We discuss two samples from STITCH-R in Section 6.
S TITCH -S shows almost no performance drop on average without adding any inference latency.
Remarkably, S TITCH -S shows an average accuracy of 78.04% (row 7), slightly lower than TBS by
1.08% (row 4), but is still much better than the ”no reasoning” baseline by 15.06% (row 2). We see
a larger performance drop in GSM8K around 8% compared with TBS, but again, still significantly
outperforms no reasoning baseline by 19.99%, again in a statistically significant way based on
McNemar’s test. Recall that the first packet latency of S TITCH -S is exactly the same as the baselines
that do not generate text reasoning; both of them only need to wait for Ntext + Nspeech tokens before
synthesizing the speech waveform. This exciting result indicates that we can improve the answer
quality without sacrificing any inference latency. By scrutinizing the responses from S TITCH -S,
we find that the first text token chunk is often a rephrase of the question, which can be generated
without any reasoning. This explains why S TITCH -S does not show a severe accuracy drop compared
with S TITCH -R.
On non-reasoning datasets, reasoning models perform comparably well or slightly outperform
non-reasoning baselines. Table 1b shows the performance on non-reasoning datasets. We find that
TBS (row 4), S TITCH -R (row 6), and S TITCH -S (row 7) all have performance comparable to or better
than the GLM-4-Voice (row 1) and the no reasoning baseline (row 2). This shows that fine-tuning
SLMs to think in text before responding does not harm the performance on non-reasoning datasets.
Generating text reasoning before generating the speech responses does not harm the perceptual
quality and the fluency of the speech response. In the rightmost block in Table 2, we report some
quality assessment of the responses generated by the SLMs. We report (1) the perceptual quality

of the speech response evaluated by UTMOSv2 (Baba et al., 2024), an automatic MOS evaluation

Human evaluation shows that STITCH-S is more responsive than STITCH-R, followed by TBS.
We conduct a human evaluation on the responsiveness of different models. We present the human
evaluator with a question from the user and two audio responses from different models, asking them
to compare the responsiveness of the two models on a seven-point scale, where +3 indicates that
model A is more responsive than model B and -3 indicates that model B is more responsive than
model A. To simulate the first packet latency that the user may experience, each audio includes a
silence in the beginning, accounting for the prefilling and decoding time of the SLM and the speech
synthesis latency. We compare the following pairs: TBS / STITCH-R, TBS / STITCH-S, STITCH-R /
STITCH-S, and STITCH / no-reasoning. For each pair, we use the same 100 questions from GSM8K,
and we recruit 3 human evaluators to judge the responsiveness of a pair of models. The results are
presented in Table 6 in the Appendix, and we summarize the results as follows: 1. STICH-R and
STITCH-S are both more responsive than TBS. 2. STITCH-S is more responsive than STITCH-R. 3.
STITCH-S and no-reasoning are equally responsive.
The reasoning segments finish much earlier than the text segments. Based on the statistics in
Table 2, we can see that the average number of reasoning chunks are less than the number of text
chunks. For example, on GSM8K, STITCH-R has on average 322.40/100 = 3.22 reasoning chunks,
while the average number of text chunks is 74.36/13 = 5.72. During inference time, we do not find
the interleaved text-speech chunks to end before the reasoning chunks finish, i.e., before the EOR is
emitted. This is mainly because when we construct the training data for STITCH-R and STITCH, we
remove the samples whose reasoning chunks are more than the text chunks, i.e., those samples that
have reasoning slower than the response. As a result, after training, the model will learn that it must
finish the reasoning before concluding its spoken response.

Q UALITATIVE R ESULTS

In this section, we provide two outputs from our S TITCH -R in Table 5 and a sample for S TITCH -S
in Table 8 in the Appendix. The top sample in Table 5 shows a typical pattern of the output from
S TITCH -R, where all the calculation results from the text token chunks are already calculated in
previous reasoning chunks. When generating the text tokens, the model simply needs to rewrite the
lengthy reasoning into more concise sentences that are easier for spoken communication.
The bottom sample in Table 5 shows a somewhat rare pattern, where the second text token chunk
includes a new calculation result (the number 120) that has not been explicitly calculated in previous
reasoning chunks. This shows that, besides thinking during the reasoning chunks, the model sometimes also incorporates the text tokens for thinking. However, this pattern is rare: We use GPT-4o to
parse the 1310 responses to GSM8K and find that only 5% of the samples illustrate this pattern. The
same pattern is also rare on S TITCH -S.
Last, we find that the first text token chunk tends to rephrase the information of the question without
adding new calculation results, which can be seen in the two samples in Table 5. This shows that the
first text token chunk can be generated without relying on additional reasoning. This can explain why
S TITCH -S can have a performance similar to TBS and S TITCH -R: since the first text token chunk
can be generated without thinking, we can directly generate those texts without conditioning on any
reasoning.

U SING R EASONING FROM OTHER M ODELS

Previously, we showed that S TITCH -R and S TITCH -S can generate reasoning before responding in
speech. Here, we discuss whether we can improve the speech response by using the reasoning from
other models. The motivation is that sometimes the reasoning generated by the SLM itself is not good
enough or takes too long to generate, and we may use other stronger or smaller models to generate the
reasoning. We call the model used to generate the reasoning the ”reasoning augmentation model”.
We discuss how to improve S TITCH -R by using a reasoning augmentation model. Given a user input
speech, we first use an ASR model to transcribe the audio and feed the transcription into the reasoning
augmentation model to generate a response, which will be used as the reasoning for S TITCH -R.
′
Next, we crop the reasoning into chunks of Ntoken
enclosed by [SOPR] and [EOPR], and then
feed the speech input and the first reasoning chunk to S TITCH -R and generate the first text-speech
interleaved token span. After the first text-speech interleaved tokens are generated, we append the
second reasoning chunk to the input and generate the second text-speech interleaved token spans. We
continue the above process to generate the full response.
In the following experiment, we replace the text reasoning of S TITCH -R using four models: GLM4-9B-Chat (GLM et al., 2024), Llama-3.1-8B, Llama-3.2-3B, and Llama-3.2-1B (Grattafiori et al.,
2024). The results are shown in Figure 3c in the Appendix, and we summarize the main findings below.
In general, stronger reasoning augmentation models (GLM-4-9B-Chat and larger Llama models)
can improve the accuracy of the spoken response, while weaker reasoning augmentation model like
Llama-3.2-1B worsens the performance of S TITCH -R. This shows that S TITCH -R can incorporate
the reasoning ability of stronger models to improve the spoken answer. The experiment also shows
that S TITCH -R really considers the contents in the reasoning to form the spoken response, instead of
independently generating the reasoning and not using that reasoning in the spoken responses.

9

C ONCLUSION

In this paper, we introduce S TITCH, a generation framework for SLM that enables simultaneous
thinking and speaking. We achieve simultaneous thinking and speaking by using the long duration
of an audio chunk to generate additional reasoning tokens before generating the speech tokens for
the next audio chunk. By design, S TITCH -S induces no additional latency compared to the models
that cannot generate reasoning before they speak. On five math reasoning QA datasets, we show
that S TITCH -R and S TITCH -S significantly improve the accuracy over non-reasoning baselines by
over 15%. On non-reasoning datasets, S TITCH -R and S TITCH -S perform comparably well with
non-reasoning baselines. Overall, the low latency and high performance of S TITCH show a promising
future direction for spoken language models.

Dialogue Datasets VoiceAssistant400K (Xiezhifei, 2024) is a spoken dialogue dataset built from a

We subsample the dataset by filtering out (1) the instances whose ‘round‘ attribute is None or 0,

generate a text reasoning given some in-context samples. GPT-4o is instructed to generate CoTs that
understand the user’s intention, recall relevant information, and provide the final answer. The prompt
we use is shown in Table 13.
Math Reasoning Datasets We include three math reasoning datasets for fine-tuning: Tulu-3Persona Math, Tulu-3-Persona-Math-Grade, and Tulu-3-Persona-Algebra. The datasets include
the question and the CoT answer in text. We directly use the ground truth CoT answer as the text
reasoning. To obtain the spoken answer, we prompt GPT-4o to rewrite the answer such that the answer
is suitable for speech communication and then synthesize it with GPT-4o-mini-TTS. The voice is
sampled from the following list: alloy, ash, ballad, coral, echo. The prompt used to rewrite the
spoken answer is shown in Table 10. The question audio is also synthesized with GPT-4o-mini-TTS,
while the list of voices we sampled from is the same as the previous. The voice of the spoken question
and response is selected randomly and independently. The total size of the math reasoning datasets
used for fine-tuning is 220K.

generate an inner monologue that understands the user’s intention, recalls relevant information, and

H UMAN E VALUATION

This section elaborates on how we conduct human evaluations to evaluate the responsiveness of
different model pairs. We conduct pairwise evaluation of the responsiveness of the following model
pairs: (1) STITCH-R vs TBS, (2) STITCH-S vs TBS, (3) STITCH-R vs STITCH-S, and (4) STITCHS vs no-reasoning. We select the above model pairs because we believe they can form a partial order
of the responsiveness of the four models.
We generate the responses of each model from the same 100 questions from GSM8K; the reason for
selecting GSM8K is that the number of reasoning tokens required for this dataset is longer, as shown
in Table 2, which may make the difference of responsiveness among the models more observable. For
each audio, we insert a silence to represent the first packet latency. The first packet latency considers
the prefilling and decoding time for the SLM, and the speech synthesis latency of the speech decoder.
The prefilling and decoding latency is profiled using an A100 GPU with vLLM, while the speech
synthesis latency is calculated based on the reported latency from Cosyvoice.
The human evaluators are given a question in GSM8K and two audios from a pair of models, and
they are asked to judge the responsiveness of the two models on a 7-point comparison category rating,
following the P.808 (Rec, 2018), using the following rubrics:
+3 : A is more responsive than B
+2 : A is somewhat responsive than B
+1 : A is slightly responsive than B
0 : A and B are equally responsive
-1 : B is slightly responsive than A
-2 : B is somewhat responsive than A
-3 : B is more responsive than A
We use Amazon MTurk (AMT) to recruit the human evaluators, and each pair of comparisons is
rated by three annotators. We require the annotators to have a HIT approval rate ≥ 98 and to hold a
master qualification on AMT. We also only include participants from the US, UK, Australia, and New
Zealand in this study. The interface for the human evaluation is shown in Figure 4. Human evaluators
are paid $1.70 for rating 8 pairs of responses, and we estimate that the hourly rate is roughly $12.75.

D ETAILED L ATENCY C ALCULATION

In the main content, we assume that the duration of each audio chunk tchunk synthesized from Nspeech
tokens is the same, and we use tchunk = 2 seconds in the paper. This is a simplification to make the
discussion easier. In fact, for the first chunk of 26 speech tokens, the corresponding audio is about 1.6
seconds, while the later audio chunks all have a duration of about 2 seconds. As a result, a safer Nreason
on an A100-80G GPU, with token per second around 86, should be 86 × 1.6 − (13 + 26) = 98.6,
which is still around 100, the number we use in our paper. It is also possible to make only the first
reasoning chunk shorter while all the later reasoning chunks have Nreason = 100. If the model is
deployed on H100-80G GPU, the token per second can be as high as 123, the maximum number for
Ntoken can be 123 × 1.6 − (13 + 26) = 157.8. Using speculative decoding (Leviathan et al., 2023) to
speed up and increase the number of reasoning tokens for each chunk is also a valid option.

F

U NSUCCESSFUL ATTEMPTS

We detail the unsuccessful attempts and negative findings observed in our experiments. Our aim is to
guide future research by highlighting these pitfalls so that others can avoid them.
LoRA Fine-tuning Initially, we tried to use LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) for fine-tuning. However, when
fine-tuning TBS using LoRA, we found that the resulting model cannot generate useful reasoning.
After fine-tuning, the performance on GSM8K is still around 35%, which is almost the same as the
accuracy of GLM-4-Voice. By observing the outputs from the fine-tuned model, we observed that the
model can generate outputs that satisfy the output format in the training data, i.e., reasoning and then
interleaved text-speech tokens. However, the reasoning is seldom correct and can make very trivial
mistakes. We suspect that this is because teaching GLM-4-Voice mathematical reasoning requires
significant model parameter updates, making LoRA fine-tuning a bad choice.
Fine-tuning only on Math Datasets We have tried to fine-tune only on the math datasets to teach
the model reasoning. However, we found that the resulting model will treat every input query as a
math question and list some equations to solve the question, even if the question simply asks ”Which
city is located at the intersection of the Tigris and Euphrates rivers?” This is possibly because the
model overfits to the pattern in the training data and forgets how to solve other non-math questions.
However, this can be easily solved by including diverse types of tasks in the training data, which is
what we do in the end.
Customized Attention Mask When training the S TITCH -R model, we tried with a variant with
customized attention masks. Precisely, we make the reasoning tokens unable to attend to the text
tokens and speech tokens, while the text and speech tokens can attend to all types of tokens. The
attention mask still remains causal. The rationale for this customized attention mask is to make the
